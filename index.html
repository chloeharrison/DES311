THE CREATIVE PROCESS

Learning Summary

In this weeks lecture I learnt about the 5 steps by James Young to help generate and solidify ideas, as well as the rituals of preparation which can help creativity. These 5 steps are:

  1. Gather raw material – Not just for the project in hand but gather material for everything that interests you.
  2. Digest what you have gathered – Once you have exhausted your research, allow time for the material to assimilate.
  3. Unconscious processing – Sleep on it and allow your subconscious do the hard work for you.
  4. Everything clicks into place – As Young calls it “a-ha! moment” when your idea seemingly comes out of thin air.
  5. Idea meets reality – As Young puts it your idea meets “the cold, grey dawn of the morning after,” and has to face reality. This is when you can seek feedback from your peers to check how robust your idea is.

Looking at the 5 steps by James Young I realise that I go through these 5 stages when I'm working on a project and really made me think on how the process that I'm going through when I am creating my own icon set and website.

Inspiration vs Imitation - Jessica Hische

“Always keep practicing (and practicing, and practicing), keep looking at beautiful work, keep researching others to look up to and be inspired by. In no time you’ll be making beautiful original work of your own.”

The last two sentences of this article stood out to me the most out of the entire article. It basically sums up every point she made about the difference between using someone else’s work for inspiration, and imitating it - a line that can be blurred especially by new designers. It’s easy to fall on to the path of just creating things that are the same as everyone else, and in some cases exactly the same - but it’s taking the time to practice away from the watchful eyes of everyone to build your skills and become just as good as your inspiration - and keeping this practice to yourself is just as important. This article really opened my eyes and taught me not to publish absolutely everything I create, even though there is an element of pressure as a website design student to constantly be creating and releasing side projects and university work. Jessica Hische reminds us that to let “practice fuel original work and then publish to your heart’s content”. She also mentions that we should diversify our inspirations, because often times our work tends to look like a combination of those who inspire us because we like their style. It’s ok to find a style that we like and stick to it, but that we should train our eyes to allow us to create original work and add our own quirks to our style.

MINIMUM VIABLE PRODUCT

Learning Summary

A Minimum viable product is a small entry basic product to see if it gathers the interest of the public. It essentially tests the market upon which we can scale based on its interest. The best example of this is a plain doughnut. If the market likes the doughnut then we can add a variety of toppings and flavours, in order to further expand the product to reach a wider audience.

In relation to my assignment deliverable, the minimum viable product will be the icons that I design and produce. Then I will create and build the promotional website which will display my icon set.

The perfect example of a MVP is Zappos. Zapoos is a website which offers the best selection of shoes in terms of brands, style and colour. It was set up in 1999 by Nick Swinmurn, after he realised that there was no major online retailer that specialized in shoes. Although Zappos wasn't a scalable business, it went on to earn $1.2 billion and became the largest online shoe store. This example demonstrates that this was a MVP experience for Swinmurn as he had an idea of a product in which he wanted to see if it would work, and it was successful.

Groupon is another massive MVP as it first started as a blog in Chicago offering half price pizzas in November 2008. It then became one of the fastest-growing companies in history. It's roughly valued at $13 billion now.

Building a lean MVP

5 main points to follow in order to build a lean MVP include:
  1. Basic static website
  2. Existing tools and platforms
  3. Email
  4. Blog
  5. Social Network

Iteration

  1. Build a rough prototype
  2. Test & gather feedback

DIGITAL PRICING MODELS

Learning Summary

To start this week’s lecture, we were told how our submissions for DES311 will be uploaded onto Blackboard which will be three URL links for the different parts of the submission which are:

  1. The promotional website
  2. The Icons
  3. The research and learning log

This week’s lecture was about the topic of Digital Pricing models. Pricing is a very critical aspect of getting your work out to the public. If priced correctly you can reach a wide audience and if people want it, you can be successful. However, incorrectly pricing your work will have the opposite effect. Overpricing will drive audiences away and under-pricing won’t help make you money. When pricing a product, you must take the quality and demand of such a product in mind. The lecture gave a good example with Apple. Apple products are typically extremely expensive compared to similar products, but people are willing to buy because they believe in the quality of Apple products. Apple’s success will only last if they are able to keep supplying the demand for high quality products. Another example of pricing given is the company Mailchimp. They offer volume sales with different price tiers. Their success depends on their customer companies sending high volumes of emails a month and paying for their service. Increased volume equals increased cost. Tiered pricing is used to give customers options such as starting low and then if they like the product they may be willing to go up a tier. The final main point of the lecture is that there is no real answer to the correct pricing. Also in the lecture we were shown a good website called UK freelance rates calculator which figures out how much you should charge for your freelance this depends on your experience and what you specialise in.

In the lecture we learnt the importance of negotiation as clients may have a different budget in mind compared to what it really costs. Also, we looked at different pricing strategies which were charge by the hour or by project. If you were to track by the hour it is important to track your time on the project by writing how long every detail of the project took to complete. Personally, I think pricing by the project is best because some projects take longer to complete compared to others and every project requires different resource, for example designing a simple leaflet doesn’t require as much time and resources compared to a website which requires a shopping cart. When pricing it is important to consider these different factors when doing a freelance job, these are:

  1. Paper
  2. Pens
  3. Photocopies
  4. Colour Copies
